001: GigGuard
002: Weekly Premium Model
003: Version 1.1 · March 2026
004: Design Philosophy
005: The GigGuard premium model is engineered from the ground up to serve the unique financial realities of a gig-economy delivery worker. Our core philosophy rejects the traditional, rigid structures of insurance in favour of a flexible, transparent, and highly aligned approach.
006: Why Weekly?
007: Gig workers live in a world of weekly, not monthly, finance. They track weekly earnings, pay for weekly expenses (like fuel), and manage their cash flow over a seven-day cycle. A monthly premium is a large, daunting commitment. A small weekly premium is a manageable operational expense, akin to topping up a fuel tank. This cadence provides unparalleled flexibility, allowing a worker to be covered during high-risk periods (like a monsoon-heavy week in Mumbai) and opt out during calmer periods, directly controlling their expenses.
008: Why Parametric Pricing?
009: Our pricing is a direct reflection of risk, calculated dynamically. Instead of a flat rate, our AI- driven model assesses the objective risk of a worker’s specific zone, the immediate weather forecast, and their personal history. This means a worker in a historically safe, dry zone pays less than one in a flood-prone area. Workers can understand why their premium might be slightly higher one week — a cyclone is forecast — and trust that it will decrease when the risk subsides.
010: Why Income Replacement?
011: We do not offer abstract, fixed-sum payouts. Our coverage is explicitly designed for income loss replacement. The payout is calculated based on the worker’s own stated average daily earnings and the duration of the disruption. If an event stops you from working for half a day, we replace the income you would have lost. This direct link between the premium paid and the real-world financial protection offered is the cornerstone of our product’s value proposition.
012: The Formula
013: The weekly premium is calculated using a multi-factor formula that balances a baseline cost with dynamic, risk-adjusted multipliers.
014: weekly_premium = base_rate × zone_multiplier × weather_multiplier × history_multiplier
015: Component
016: Range
017: Description
018: base_rate
019: ₹35 (fixed)
020: Fixed cost covering minimum operational expenses: API costs, server hosting, transaction fees. Represents the absolute floor price under ideal conditions.
021: zone_multiplier
022: 0.80 – 1.40
023: AI-computed long-term risk for the worker’s specific geographic zone, trained on historical weather and disruption data. Flood-prone coastal zones approach 1.40; dry inland zones fall below 1.0.
024: weather_multiplier
025: 0.90 – 1.30
026: Short-term forward-looking adjustment based on the 7-day OpenWeatherMap forecast. Elevated when multiple days of >10mm rain or a heatwave are predicted; reduced to 0.90 during clear, calm forecasts.
027: history_multiplier
028: 0.85 – 1.25
029: Personalises the premium based on claims history. New workers start at 1.0. Three consecutive claim-free months reduces the multiplier by 0.05 (floor: 0.85). More than 3 claims per month raises it (ceiling: 1.25).
030: Coverage Amount Calculation
031: coverage_amount = avg_daily_earning × (disruption_hours / 8) × 0.8
032: The 80% replacement rate is standard insurance practice. It creates a co-participation in the loss that reduces moral hazard (the temptation not to work even if safe to do so) and keeps premiums affordable for everyone.
033: A hard weekly cap of ₹800 per worker limits catastrophic exposure during multi-day events such as week-long floods. This cap is the primary risk control that makes the financial model sustainable at scale.

173: Business Viability
174: Target Loss Ratio: 65%
175: Our goal is to maintain a loss ratio where for every ₹100 collected in premiums, a maximum of
176: ₹65 is paid out in claims. The remaining 35% is allocated to operational costs, transaction fees, and a sustainable profit margin. The dynamic multipliers are the primary instrument for maintaining this ratio across the annual cycle.

191: Annual Reserve Accumulation Model
192: The loss ratio is not uniform throughout the year. Monsoon season (June–September) and winter smog season (November–January) generate elevated claim rates, while the remaining weeks are low-claim. The reserve model shows how surplus from quiet weeks funds the high- payout periods to balance out at the target 65% annual loss ratio.

251: premium must be off by no more than a few rupees from the ideal risk price.

286: Training Data
287: A synthetic dataset of 10,000+ entries is generated from approximately 50–100 defined zones across the five cities. Historical API data populates the features. Target risk scores are hand- crafted from known ground truths (Mumbai monsoon zones score high; elevated Bangalore zones score low). The training pipeline is fully scripted and reproducible from the repository.
