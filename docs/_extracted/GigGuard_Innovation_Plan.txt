038: Hexagonal Geospatial Indexing
039: What the original technique does
040: Uber's H3 system divides the entire Earth into a hierarchical grid of hexagonal cells. At resolution 8 — which Uber uses for its city-level demand modeling — each hexagon covers approximately 0.74 km². Because hexagons tile perfectly with no gaps and have equal distance from center to every edge, they eliminate the directional bias that rectangular grids introduce. Each cell has a unique 64-bit integer ID that encodes its position, resolution, and parents/children in the hierarchy. Uber uses H3 to localize surge pricing, driver positioning, and demand prediction to sub-kilometer precision — something impossible with vague text zone names like "South Delhi."
041: Why GigGuard needs it
042: GigGuard's current schema stores zone as a free-text VARCHAR. This creates the biggest financial risk in any parametric insurance system: basis risk — paying workers who were not actually in the affected area. When OpenWeatherMap reports heavy rain at a GPS coordinate, we currently map it to the entire "Lajpat Nagar zone" — which might span 12 km². Workers at the dry end of that zone get paid for rain they never experienced. H3 eliminates this. A rain event has a precise lat/lng coordinate, which we convert to an H3 hex ID. We then pay workers whose stored home hex falls within that hex's k=1 ring (the hex plus its 6 immediate neighbors — covering roughly a 2 km radius). Nothing outside that ring gets triggered.

120: Contextual Bandits for Policy Recommendation
121: What the original technique does
122: Netflix personalizes the artwork thumbnail shown for each title using contextual bandits — specifically, a Thompson Sampling algorithm. Each thumbnail variant is an "arm." The context is the user's viewing history, time of day, and device. The bandit continuously explores which arm maximizes play-rate for each context cluster, balancing exploration (trying new variants) vs. exploitation (showing the known best). This eliminated the need for manual A/B test scheduling — the bandit runs 24/7, updating its belief about each arm's expected reward after every impression.
123: Why GigGuard needs it
124: GigGuard currently offers the same policy to every worker: ₹35 base rate, same coverage tiers. But a Swiggy worker in Delhi during monsoon has a completely different risk appetite than a Zomato worker in Chennai in February. A contextual bandit learns which coverage tier (₹29 / ₹44 / ₹65 / ₹89) each worker segment is most likely to purchase — and surfaces that tier first in the onboarding UI. Over time, it discovers non-obvious correlations: maybe experienced workers (avg_daily_hours > 8) strongly prefer higher coverage, while new workers are price-sensitive. No analyst has to define these segments manually.

144: Algorithm design
145: Thompson Sampling is the algorithm of choice because it handles cold-start gracefully (uniform Beta(1,1) prior means all arms are equally likely until data accumulates) and is trivially parallelizable. Each arm k has a Beta distribution Beta(alpha_k, beta_k). To select an arm, sample from each distribution — the arm with the highest sample wins. After observing a reward r, update: alpha_k += r, beta_k += (1 - r).

197: Reinforcement Learning Premium Engine
198: What the original technique does
199: Uber uses an off-policy deep RL algorithm (TD3 — Twin Delayed DDPG) for surge pricing. The agent observes a state vector (supply, demand, time, location cluster) and outputs a price multiplier. The reward is a composite: driver earnings + rider completion rate + marketplace efficiency. Crucially, the agent learns that pricing too high collapses demand (no riders), and pricing too low is unprofitable. No human needs to hand-tune the multipliers — the agent discovers the optimal balance through millions of simulated episodes before going live.
200: Why GigGuard needs it
201: GigGuard's Phase 1 premium formula is: weekly_premium = 35 × zone_multiplier × weather_multiplier
202: × history_multiplier. These multipliers are hand-crafted. If zone_multiplier for Dharavi is set to 1.4 but the actual claim rate there is 0.8×, GigGuard overcharges workers and loses them to competitors. An RL agent replaces this formula entirely. It learns: "for this state (zone risk, forecast, worker history, competitor pricing), what premium maximizes both purchase rate AND loss ratio below 80%?" The agent self-corrects weekly as new claim data arrives.

256: Graph Neural Network Fraud Detection
257: What the original technique does
258: Stripe's Radar fraud system builds a dynamic heterogeneous graph connecting cards, devices, merchants, email addresses, and IP ranges. Even if a single fraudulent account looks clean in isolation, a Graph Neural Network (GNN) flags it because it shares graph edges with known-bad entities. The GNN aggregates neighborhood information — "your neighbors are suspicious, therefore your risk score is elevated" — which is fundamentally impossible for any model that looks at one account at a time.
259: Why GigGuard needs it
260: GigGuard's Phase 1 fraud scorer is Isolation Forest — a model that evaluates each worker's claim independently. This misses the most common real-world fraud pattern: a ring of 10–20 workers who all claim the same "disruption event" from the same neighborhood, all registered from the same device, and all receiving payouts to the same UPI VPA. Isolation Forest sees 10 individually-normal claims. A GNN sees the ring immediately — because the graph edge "shares_upi_with" creates a dense cluster of high-risk nodes.

330: Causal Inference Claim Validation
331: What the original technique does
332: Netflix uses difference-in-differences (DiD) and doubly-robust estimators to separate "the recommendation caused this watch" from "the user was going to watch anyway." Without causal inference, Netflix would over-credit its recommendation system — seeing correlation and calling it causation. Spotify applies the same logic to playlist performance: did featuring a song cause streams, or did popular songs get featured? The academic foundation is the Rubin Potential Outcomes framework — every observed outcome has a counterfactual ("what would have happened otherwise?").
333: Why GigGuard needs it
334: The most expensive false positive in parametric insurance is paying a worker who was already offline before the trigger event. Consider: a worker whose phone died at 3pm, who then claims a 4pm rainstorm caused their income loss. GigGuard's current system pays based on trigger alone — no counterfactual check. Causal inference adds one question before every payout: "Would this worker have been working if the trigger hadn't happened?" We estimate this using their historical activity on the same weekday at the same time — the difference-in-differences counterfactual. Only the causal income loss gets paid.

408: Smart Contract Auto-Execution
409: What the original technique does
410: AXA's Fizzy platform (2017) was the insurance industry's first live blockchain parametric product. Flight delay insurance was encoded as an Ethereum smart contract. When the Flightradar24 data oracle confirmed a delay >= 2 hours, the contract self-executed — calling the payout function directly, with no
411: human in the loop and no claim form. The contract was immutable: AXA literally could not deny a valid claim once deployed. This is the gold standard for trust and transparency in parametric insurance.
412: Why GigGuard needs it
413: GigGuard's target users — delivery workers earning ₹600–800/day — have zero reason to trust a new insurance platform. "Trust us, we'll pay automatically" is a marketing claim. An on-chain policy is a mathematical guarantee. The worker's policy terms, UPI address, and payout conditions are encoded in an immutable Solidity contract. When Chainlink's weather oracle confirms rain > 15mm/hr at the worker's H3 hex, the contract calls Razorpay directly. No human can block the payout. Workers can verify their own policy on Etherscan. This is the feature that converts the most skeptical user.
